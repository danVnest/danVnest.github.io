    Sunbar: the sunrise alarm clock

    It is almost never pleasant to jarringly wake up to the sound of a normal alarm clock. With the Sunbar, you don't have to. It wakes you up naturally, just as a sunrise does. By introducing a gradually brightening light your body is stimulated to enter a lighter sleep pattern, resulting in an easier awakening. You all know what it feels like to wake up from deep sleep, and noone likes that groggy, disorientated feeling. If you want to know more about sleep patterns and the affects of light upon it, read the introduction from my university thesis, which can be downloaded from here.

    I have developed the first prototype of this device, which is functional - and actually more effective that I had reason to expect. I am collecting data and user feedback, while also developing the second prototype, which will be utilised to advertise and drive presales, and in turn will fund large-scale production.

    I won't describe the Sunbar's functionality in detail just yet, but know that most importantly it is reliable to wake with, and it works for couples.

    Come back soon for more info!

    University Thesis: Automated System for Optimal Human Awakening.

    The detection of human sleeping patterns with small, non-invasive devices is an emerging technology. At the time of writing, some medical grade devices were available (expensive), and a couple of consumer devices were emerging (yet defective or inadequate i.e. WakeMate and Jawbone UP).

    Almost all of these devices used only accelerometer data to detect sleep patterns, with moderate to good results. However my thesis focused on the fusion of this data with skin temperature and heart rate data (both of which had not been used before in this manner), in order to improve the accuracy of the sleep pattern analysis.

    UPDATE: some current devices are using these same methods now similar methods, for example the Apple Watch is using a reflectance heart rate monitor design like I did (one of my biggest regrets about my thesis is not talking to these big companies about it).

    In addition, I looked at how simple stimuli (primarily light) can affect human sleep patterns to induce a more peaceful awakening.

    You can see the case above, which contains a microprocessor, bluetooth module, battery, accelerometer, vibration motor, and interconnecting circuitry. On the outside of the case you can see thermally conductive silicone paste, which conceals a temperature sensor, and holds the light reflectance based heart rate monitor. The case fits inside a comfortable strap on your wrist. Of course this prototype is much larger than what a final consumer product could be.

    The results of the thesis were promising. I showed that the extra sensory information could be used to form a relatively accurate approximation of your sleep state. You can view the entire thesis here. I found that using light to influence sleep behaviour was quite effective, and have since been developing a sunrise alarm clock which is discussed here.

    Perspective Three Dimensional Imaging (P3DI)

    The purpose of this project was to present a 3D object on a 2D screen, where the viewer can manipulate the perspective of the image shown simply by moving their head. Essentially we were mimicking how one can view different aspects of a real 3D object by looking at it from different angles. Thus there were two main technical components to develop: detecting the position of the viewer's head, and adjusting the image shown on the screen accordingly.

    My colleague Satveer Singh worked mainly on the detection process, utilising various computer vision methods from the OpenCV framework. I helped him map the coordinates of the head detected into a model of the environment used to construct the image on the screen. The model consisted of a box in the computer screen...

    This project was dreamed up as part of the Experimental Robotics course at the University of Sydney.

    Inverted Pendulum: an educational tool

    This device is quite simple, consisting of a horizontal slide with a platform that can move along it. The platform has a motor connected to a gear rack parallel to the slide so the platform can move itself along the slide. Hanging freely from the platform is a pendulum, so as the platform moves the pendulum will swing. By implementing some state-space control methods and a starting sequence the platform can move to swing the pendulum up into a vertical position and balance it there.

    I made this device for two reasons, firstly because it is awesome and I wanted to see if I could make it, and secondly to use for educational purposes. As part of the Advanced Engineering - Techology Education course at uni we taught high school kids about engineering. Each member of my group for this project tought about their own specialisation, mine being mechatronics. I structured the workshop to move naturally from the physics and maths the students already knew, to control theory and mechatronics in general. While an inverted pendulum is quite an abstract device, the technology behind it has applications in numerous control systems, from Segways to airplane autopilots/stabilisers - plus it might have inspired some future engineers.

    Dangerous Driving Detection

    For this project we were tasked by the Warren Centre with the broad challenge of "reducing road fatalities". After much brainstorming and analysis of background research into existing project, we came too the conclusion that gathering more information about driving behavour was critical - or more importantly, developing a system to gather more informative data for that purpose.

    We needed a way to gather sensory data from large samples of the population. As the majority of the driving population owns a smartphone, we developed an app that utilised the phones on-board sensors to detect certain driving patterns. While the purpose of this project was primarily to develop a proof of concept, we also researched and structured a business case to incentivise users to use the app (such as rewards, lower insurance costs for safe drivers, and social integration), and allow commercialisation of the idea.

    A couple of years after this project was finished, I noticed a company had formed with an incredibly similar business model: www.automatic.com.

    I was solely responsible for developing the app itself, as I had previous experience developing on iOS (I developed an innovative game). We tested it on the road, and validated its results in controlled environments. The app could clearly identify dangerous driving situations, such as excessive breaking, swerving and fast lane changes.
